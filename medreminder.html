    Medication Reminder UX

    Duration: 2019 (1months)

    My Role: UI/UX Designer

    A system for tracking and improving medication adherence

    notification_important
    To comply with my non-disclosure agreement, I have omitted and obfuscated confidential information in this case study. All information in this case study is my own and does not necessarily reflect the views of Verily.

    Background

    Verily is building a solution to help patients track and improve their medication adherence. While we were planning on doing first round usability testing, I was in charge of designing the user experiene of onboarding and "Create a Reminder" flows for the planned study.

    The Team

    1 designer, 1 product manager, 1 industrial designer, 1 software engineer, 2 hardware engineers 2 UX researchers.

    My Role as UI/UX Designer

    • Collaborated with the cross functional team to create end-to-end experience including concept ideation, prototyping, and production-ready designs
    • Collaborated with the partner’s UX team to use and expand the component library and brand usage
    • Provided specs and assets for the implementation

    The Problems

    • 75% of patients fail to take their medications as prescribed, which leads to 33% of prescriptions never being filled and $100 billion in avoidable hospitalizations.
    • Patients are unaware of missed doses.
    • Pharmacists are unaware to what happens once medication leaves the store.

    Unique Value Proposition

    For patients who desire to take their medications at the right time and are open to personalization, we can provide continuous support to help them stay on track.



    Challenges

    • To build the app on top of the existing Pill Reminder app, it was crucial to understand the product’s ecosystem. There was a limited room for explorations in terms of UX & UI pattern.
    • There was no integration between the hardware device set up and the existing prescription setup process.
    • Patients should be able to set up multiple medications that may not have the hardware attached.
    • There was no user-friendly way for patients to view dose history.

    Targeted User Personas

    Age:

    65+

    Personal Device:

    Smartphone user

    Medical Condition:

    United Health Care Plan with PDC<= Tier 2 in disease states of Diabetes, Hypertension or Colesterol

    Lifestyle:

    independent, open to use technology, willing to wel-informed, and desire to take medications that are prescribed.

    Research

    Before diving into the mock-up interface, I wanted to study the problem space and how our competitors are handling the features (baseline & bonus). As I collaborated with the researchers, we could identify opportunities in medication adherence that are not being addressed by current solutions.


    Key takeaways from the landscape analysis:

    • Most solutions address barriers to access, but do NOT address underlying motivational or belief factors that are the bulk of reasons for non-adherence. Additionally, most solutions only solve for one category of needs.
    • Nonadherence is more complicated than addressing forgetfulness. It involves: relationship(improve trust, communication, and relationship with care team), motivation(what is it for me?) and access (remove barriers, make it easy)
    1. Private notifications that do not display medication names
    2. Flexible scheduling with “as needed”, days intervals, and fixed scheduling options
    3. Ability to take, skip or snooze/postpone medication reminder
    4. Detailed history of meds taken, skipped and/or missed, including overall adherence
    5. Information storage for personal health metrics, pharmacies and doctors
    6. Multiple medication types accepted for input, with option to customize size, shape & color
    7. Drug database that auto-populates a list of suggestions as you type
    8. Detailed dosage and quantity
    9. Refill tracking and alert when medications are running low
    10. Pin protection option
    1. Gamification features such as earning points & rewards when adhering to medication schedule(Mango health)
    2. Option to take a real photo of medication
    3. Take photos of prescription label to automatically import all relevant information including refill date, dosage, doctor, etc.
    4. Integrate into existing routines; HERO pill dispenser can go on the counter next to your coffee maker! medication reminder
    5. Non-app reminders such as the Adhere Tech smart bottle that lights up or chimes when you forget to take a med, and automatically sends data

    Audit: Existing Prescription Setup Flow

    To better understand the ecosystem of the partner's product, I walked through the 'Create a Reminder' flow from onboarding. I could identify the best place within the flow where we can insert the new pairing flow. Likewise, I learned the fundamental design language that set up in the interface.

    Existing flow of creating a reminder

    Ideation

    From the brainstorming session, we came up with several options for the history screen. Analyzing competitor’s UX patterns and research insights, we learned that people use dashboards to track todo lists and their health data. Hence, we wanted to make the home screen a place where users can create and track their medications, so we explored multiple versions which will be simple and straightforward for our end users to navigate through.

    Tested V1 flow above

    Testing Round 1 & Iterations

    People were NOT clear about the concept and value proposition. From the first-round testing, we found that we did not clearly convey why & how the new hardware device works for users. We decided to add an in-app tutorial to help patients understand set up & use of the hardware device.

    In-app tutorial iterations

    Iconography & Reminder Functionality

    • Green was a nice positive reinforcement that you’re on the right track
    • Many were unsure what the yellow icon represented. Once explained, it was unclear whether this state was useful
    • The pill icon wasn’t understood but people could assume that represent upcoming medications with the context
    Scroll animation

    The legend shrinks on scroll to save space


    History
    From research, we learned that:

    • Most liked seeing the detailed view for each day below the weekly view
    • Anticipated looking at it in the evening to see if they were on track, or midday to check if they missed doses
    • For most, Monthly View was a bit overwhelming and not as action-oriented as the weekly/daily view
    • “Take” and “Skip” were most useful for meds to still have days around whether or not the dose was taken

    Per users' feedback, I suggested simplifying the monthly view and making it secondary. Our users mainly refer to today's reminders and the weekly history view to track how they are doing.     Create a Reminder Flow for FTU

    For initial launch, the experience had to encompass various scenarios of use. This includes for first-time users and for users who don't have certain settings like notification, camera access, or Bluetooth on.

    Next Steps

    Specs & Assets
    First, I handed off the ux specifications and assets for the implementation process. I documented detailed interaction behaviors along with the screen flows (the ‘Create a Reminder’ flow for both FTU & existing users) in a shared deck, and then added a link to the drive folder that contains all the assets (icons, illustrations, images, etc.).

    Work on Edge Case Scenarios
    "What if a user's smartphone lost the hardware connection?" Or "What if the hardware device failed to send data?" Moving forward, we should work more on use case scenarios to identify potential user needs and breaks in the user experience.

    Reflect

    Working on this joint project with the partner company, I learned to stay flexible and prioritize ideas to design a user experience that helps both organizations achieve their goals while maximizing usability for our end users. Making intended design decision was key to create convincing and well-thought-out UX that can win for all (businesses & users).
