    Medication Reminder UX

    Duration: 2019 (1months)

    My Role: UI/UX Designer

    A system for tracking and improving medication adherence

    notification_important
    To comply with my non-disclosure agreement, I have omitted and obfuscated confidential information in this case study. All information in this case study is my own and does not necessarily reflect the views of Verily or Walgreens.

    The Problems

    • 75% of patients fail to take their medications as prescribed, which leads to 33% of prescriptions never being filled and $100 billion in avoidable hospitalizations.
    • Patients are unaware of missed doses.
    • Pharmacists are blind to what happens once medication leaves the store.

    Background

    Verily is building a hardware device, an app, and a console at the pharmacy to help patients track and improve their medication adherence. This project was still in the early development stage that required rapid design and validation through research and testing to deliver the app for a trial with Walgreens in early next year. Although the new flow will eventually be implemented to the Walgreens app, we needed to build a separate mobile app for this trial. While Walgreens was planning on doing first round usability testing, I was in charge of helping them design the app’s UX for the planned study.

    The Team

    1 experience designer, 1 product manager, 1 industrial designer, 1 software engineer, 2 hardware engineers 2 UX researchers.

    My Role as solo UX designer

    • Collaborated with the cross functional team to create end-to-end experience including concept ideation, prototyping, and production-ready designs.
    • Collaborated with the partner’s ux team to use and expand the component library and brand usage
    • Provided specs and assets for the implementation

    Unique Value Proposition

    For patients that desire to take their medications at the right time and are open to personalized, continual support to help them stay on track.



    Program Touchpoints

    Challenges

    • Building the app on top of the existing Walgreens Pill Reminder app: Understanding the product’s ecosystem was crucial. There was a limited room for explorations in terms of UX & UI pattern.
    • Finding a way to integrate the cap set up into the existing prescription setup process. The team would like patients to be able to set up multiple medications that may not have a smart cap attached.
    • Create a dose history view and help thinking about the best way to implement that.

    MVP Goals

    • Have user agree to study requirements
    • Allow a user to setup and connect to the single or multiple smart hardware(s)
    • Send dose reminders for each medication
    • Send dose history for each medication
    • Send data to Walgreens

    Research

    Before diving into mocking up the interface, I wanted to study the problem space and how our competitors are handling the features (baseline & bonus). Collaborating with the researchers, we could identify opportunities in medication adherence that are not being addressed by current solutions.


    Key takeaways from landscape analysis were:

    • Most solutions address barriers to access, but do NOT address underlying motivational or belief factors that are the bulk of reasons for non-adherence. Additionally, most solutions only solve for one category of needs.
    • Nonadherence is more complicated than addressing forgetfulness. It involves: relationship(improve trust, communication, and relationship with care team), motivation(what is it for me?) and access (remove barriers, make it easy)
    1. Private notifications that do not display medication names
    2. Flexible scheduling with “as needed”, days intervals, and fixed scheduling options
    3. Ability to take, skip or snooze/postpone medication reminder
    4. Detailed history of meds taken, skipped and/or missed, including overall adherence
    5. Information storage for personal health metrics, pharmacies and doctors
    6. Multiple medication types accepted for input, with option to customize size, shape & color
    7. Drug database that auto-populates a list of suggestions as you type
    8. Detailed dosage and quantity
    9. Refill tracking and alert when medications are running low
    10. Pin protection option
    1. Gamification features such as earning points & rewards when adhering to medication schedule(Mango health)
    2. Option to take a real photo of medication
    3. Take photo of prescription label to automatically import all relevant information including refill date, dosage, doctor, etc.
    4. Integrate into existing routines; HERO pill dispenser can go on the counter next to your coffee maker! medication reminder
    5. Non-app reminders such as the Adhere Tech smart bottle that lights up or chimes when you forget to take a med, and automatically sends data

    Design Principles

    We should design for:
    1. People's belief and values
    2. Demonstrating the value of taking medications
    3. Giving patients confidence and peace of mind
    4. A constellation of needs (not just one or two)

    Audit: Existing Prescription Setup Flow

    To understand Walgreens Pill Reminder app’s ecosystem, I walked through the flow from onboarding to get a sense of how the app works and users’ expectations toward the product. Also, I could identify where might be the best place within the flow to insert the new paring flow. Likewise, I learned the fundamental design language that set up in the interface.

    Existing flow of creating a reminder in Walgreens app

    Brainstorming with stakeholders

    From the brainstorming session, we came up with several options for the history screen. Analyzing competitor’s UX patterns and research insights, we learned that people use dashboard to track todo lists and their health data. We wanted to make the home screen a place where users can create and track their medications, so we explored multiple versions that might be simple and straightforward for our end users to navigate through.

    Testing Round 1 & Iterations

    People were NOT clear about the concept and value proposition. From the first-round testing, we found that we did not clearly convey why & how the new hardware device works for users. We decided to add an in-app tutorial to help patients understand set up & use of the hardware device.

    Tested V1 flow above


    Moving forward, the tutorial should include:

    • How the button works and when to press it to activate the hardware device
    • How to read the “hours since last dose”
    • How Bluetooth technology works & out of sync
    • How Bluetooth range affects usage
    • Why the hardware needs to be returned at every refill
    • What happens to personal data when cap is returned


    History

    • Most liked seeing the detailed view for each day below the weekly view
    • Anticipated looking at it in the evening to see if they were on track, or midday to check if they missed doses
    • For most, Monthly View was a bit overwhelming and not as actionable as the weekly/daily view



    Iconography & Reminder Functionality

    • Green was a nice positive reinforcement that you’re on the right track
    • Many were unsure what the yellow icon represented. Once explained, it was unclear whether this state was useful
    • The pill icon wasn’t understood but people could assume that represent upcoming medications with the context
    • “Take” and “Skip” were most useful for meds to still have daya around whether or not the dose was taken

    Impact

    Next Steps

    Specs & Assets
    First, I handed off the ux specifications and assets for the implementation process. I documented detailed interaction behaviors along with the screen flows (the ‘Create a Reminder’ flow for both FTU & existing users) in a shared deck, and then added a link to the drive folder that contains all the assets (icons, illustrations, images, etc.).

    Features & Hardware function
    Based on learnings from these studies, the team decided to improve function of the hardware. Right now, we rely heavily on the app to send reminders. However, we learned that our user group tend to expect the actual smart hardware device to be interactive. For example, they would love to see it also makes sound at scheduled time.

    Reflect

    Working on this joint project with partner company, I learned to stay flexible and prioritize ideas to design a user experience that helps both organizations achieve their goals while maximizing usability. Making intended design decision was key to create convincing and well-thought-out UX that can win for all (businesses & users).
