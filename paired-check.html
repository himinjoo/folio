    Blood Sugar Monitoring Exercise

    Duration: 2018-2019

    My Role: UI/UX Designer

    A habit forming exercise to help diabetic patients stay in healthy range

    notification_important
    To comply with my non-disclosure agreement, I have omitted and obfuscated confidential information in this case study. All information in this case study is my own and does not necessarily reflect the views of Verily.

    Background

    Onduo, a joint venture between Verily and Sanofi, is a virtual diabetes clinic. Through the app, patients have access to lessons and activities that were designed to understand the impact of food and exercise on blood glucose. This Paired Check exercise provides sufficient information to alert both the patient and their care team in the app if their diabetes control is declining, or to reinforce good behavior if they’re maintaining good glycemic control.

    The Team

    3 designers, 1 product manager, 2 engineers, 1 behavioral scientists, 2 UX researchers

    My Role as UI/UX Designer

    I inherited this project from designers who previously worked on the first version Paired Check Challenge. Collaborating with cross-functional teams, I contributed to the next version Paired Check Challenge by executing ideas, customer journey map, wireframes, prototypes, icons, illustration, etc.

    What if managing blood sugar monitoring became simple and useful?

    Introducing Paired Check Challenge

    Healthy eating is a cornerstone of healthy living — with or without diabetes. But if you have diabetes, you need to know how foods affect your blood sugar levels. It's not only the type of food you eat but also how much you eat and the combinations of food types you eat. For our users, we can provide more valuable insights on meal choice and allow them to track trends over time in their condition.

    Targeted User Personas

    Type 2 diabetes is the most common type of diabetes. As I briefly mentioned above, keeping the blood sugar(BS) in the safe range is crucial for diabetic patients. However, our users find it's quite difficult to keep on track with the BS checks due to various reasons. I used these two personas to keep myself aware of our user's needs and potential pain points while exploring new solutions.

    Understanding the Problem Space

    To have the better picture of what problem I was trying to solve, I started learning about how this feature can help our users with diabetes. Working in the healthcare industry, I have become comfortable with designing for unfamilar topics. Mostly, there is quite a bit of learning curve upfront, but it is certainly essential to create the right product. Also, I can always reach out to clinicians and researchers for an help to hear their insights on what it is like to live with a certain type of disease.

    Learn from the Previous Version(V1)

    Based off of the previously conducted diary study, I approached this problem from analyzing success and failures. This research was a major breakthrough for me and allowed me to focus the energy on creating the right experience, this time. Key takeaways that we found were:

    1. Users' schedules are the root cause of people having difficulty.
    2. Functionality does not accommodate user schedules resulting in usability issues.
    3. Value drivers are not emphasized.

    Old Experience | Customer Journey Map

    Then, I had more clear vision in mind. To create more focused design solutions, I set the design principles to move forward with the process. The key design principles to keep in mind were:

    • Personalized: By providing opportunities for personalization, people can discover tailored food habits.
    • Asisstive: By helping people to keep on track and reflect what & how they comsume, patients can build healthier haibts.
    • Positive: By projecting a positive tone with the interactions and design intervention, participants are subconsciously influenced to hold a positive attitude about the activity.
    • Simple Interactions: By utilizing simple interactions to reduce cognitive load, elderly patients can easily and intuitively move through the journey.

    Next, I moved forward by focusing on the strengths and opportunities of the approach to distinguish between where we are now and where we wish to be.

    After analyzing the strengths, it seemed logical to use the existing Challenge Framework we designed for other behavior modification and skill building exercises.

    Challenge Framework

    As you can see the wireframe below, the flow begins with setting up proper expectations prior to ask people to try it out, and then they can schedule it at their convenient time. At the end of the activity, we provide them with useful insights that our members can take away with. Moving forward, I continued to retain that “Give-and-Take” rule.

    Redesign the In-Progress Card

    Although I used the existing framework for the beginning part, there were still many areas which required customizations for the feature specific. For example, the dashboad in-progress card was one of them. As diving deeper into it, I started asking myself questions:

    • How can I bring 'give-and-take' methodology to this card?
    • Why is this card valuable for our users?
    • How can we provide flexibility?
    • How can we prevent cancellation?
    • How might we motivate our users to log their meals with full descriptions?
    • Then, how might we simplify the logging flow?

    After analyzing pros and cons of previous in-progress card, I started brainstorming. Although I planned to retain things that work well, I’d like to explore more options that would be more effective and intuitive.

    First, a question came to my mind was that if we want to allow people to manually input their blood sugar(BS) measurement, although data automatically synced. My gut feeling said we should enable the manual log because there was time when the meter failed to sync data. I moved forward with both options: 1) BS data gets only updated automatically. In other words, users can only manually log their meals. 2) Manual logging is available until Paired Check is completed.

    Then, I dive into Sketch where I can move things around more quickly. I kept them low-fidelity till then because I wanted to receive other falks’ feedback while it’s still in early stage.

    Then, I dive into Sketch where I can more quickly move things around. I kept them low-fidelity till then because I want to hear other falks’ opinions & feedback.

    The experience had to encompass various scenarios. We ask questions if our users fail to log food or take readings to collect useful data for improvement. This was a good learning experience for me to come up with an efficient flow for every case.

    Impact

    From the last usability test, all 7 participants could successfully finished Paired Check Challenge and shared positive feedback. Most participants, even those who have never completed challenges before, expressed interest in trying the paired check.

    Next Steps

    Custom Care Pack

    Per our users feedback, we found that people want to learn more about healthy foods and receipes. I took a stab at the early explorations of the healthy package combining with other existing Onduo Lessons and Activities.

    Lesson Learned

    More context the better

    Never assume they will know what they see without explanation. Providing proper context upfront is crucial for our users, and there are various ways to introduce that information depending on the case.


    Setting the right tone is crucial

    UX writing bridges a gap between the visual solutions and the usability, between the product and its users. It isn't just about how to write, but it's also about how we set the right tone.
